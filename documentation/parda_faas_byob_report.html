Executive Summary

This report sets out a business case for turning Parda Faas (पर्दाफास), the civic investigation platformer built for the Enterprise Project module, into a product that earns money. The engagement that the game produces through its gamification layer is treated here as a commercial asset. A freemium model was selected as the most appropriate structure for a browser-based product targeting Nepali young adults and the wider South Asian diaspora. Revenue would be earned through three streams: a voluntary supporter subscription, civic-mission-aligned in-game purchases, and carefully placed advertising that does not break the play experience.

The target market was studied, three distinct player groups were identified, and a lean marketing plan was built around the platforms where those groups are already active. A launch cost breakdown was prepared and kept deliberately modest, with the largest single item being the marketing and community-building spend. A cautious revenue projection places break-even at approximately month six after public launch. The ethical complications of earning money from a game that also teaches civic values are addressed directly rather than being postponed, because they bear on every decision in the model.

1. Introduction

A game that holds attention every day is a game that can be built into a business, and this report sets out how that would be done for Parda Faas. The game was described in full in the Enterprise Project report, where the focus was placed on how its gamification layer produces engagement. Here the focus shifts to what that engagement is worth and how it can be turned into a product that pays for itself and then earns beyond its costs.

Parda Faas occupies a position that few browser games hold. It is, at the same time, a game, a civic education tool, and a form of investigative satire. That combination is commercially unusual and creates both advantages and complications. The advantages are that the game carries inherent social value, which makes it easier to talk about and to fund through non-commercial sources as well as commercial ones. The complications are that the same mechanics that create engagement also raise ethical questions when money is attached to them, and those questions are given space in this report rather than being minimised.

As with the Enterprise Project report, the figures used here are illustrative. They are built on stated assumptions, and those assumptions are exposed rather than hidden so that the reasoning can be evaluated on its own terms.

2. Motivation

The group was motivated by a practical question: how does a project with civic purpose survive beyond its first funding? Nepal has no shortage of applications, websites, and community campaigns that address corruption and accountability. Most of them run for a year or two and then disappear because there is no income to sustain the people working on them. Parda Faas was designed from the start with this problem in mind, and the business model in this report is a direct response to it. A product that earns its own way does not depend on grant cycles or political goodwill to continue existing.

A second motivation came from the specific shape of the game's audience. Young people between eighteen and twenty-eight in Nepal and in the Nepali diaspora are among the most active mobile and browser game players in the region, and they are also the group most likely to discuss politics online. A game that sits at the intersection of those two habits occupies a commercially interesting position, and working out what that position is worth was judged to be a useful exercise in its own right.

4. The Product

Parda Faas is a browser-based, keyboard-controlled civic investigation platformer. The player chooses one of seven investigator characters, each modelled as a satirical caricature of a real Nepali public figure. They move through a level representing a tier of Nepal's governance, collecting evidence, talking to witness NPCs, rejecting bribe offers, and eventually publishing a newspaper exposé. There are five levels of increasing difficulty, from a ward councillor's ghost project at the easiest end to a federal cabinet minister at the hardest. The game runs in any modern browser without installation, which removes the single largest friction point for a new player trying the product for the first time.

From a commercial perspective, the most valuable feature of the product is the daily return habit that the gamification layer creates. The streak mechanic, the rank progression system, and the timed case files all work to bring a player back the following day. A player who returns daily is a player who can be offered something every day, and the business model is built on that fact. The civic subject matter adds a second layer of commercial value: partnerships with journalism schools, civil society organisations, and government transparency initiatives are plausible in a way that would not apply to a generic platformer, and these open funding channels that do not exist for most small games.

5. Business Model and Monetisation

A freemium model was selected. The game is free to access and free to play in its entirety, which keeps the barrier to entry at zero. Income is earned from players who choose to pay and from advertising shown to players who do not. This model fits the target audience well: young adults in Nepal and the South Asian diaspora are familiar with freemium products, and the audience size is large enough that even a small spending rate across a large number of players produces useful income.

Three revenue streams were planned, and they are shown in Figure 2. The first is a voluntary supporter subscription, priced at NPR 299 per month, which unlocks a supporter badge visible to other players, removes all advertising, and grants early access to new levels. The word "supporter" was chosen deliberately rather than "premium" or "pro," because the civic purpose of the game makes an appeal to shared values more commercially effective than an appeal to exclusive features. The second stream is civic-mission-aligned in-game purchases. Players can buy a "Source Shield" that protects a witness from being silenced for one level, an "Evidence Vault" that stores extra evidence slots, or a "Deadline Extension" that adds three minutes to a timed case. These items are framed entirely within the game's investigative vocabulary rather than as generic currency. The third stream is advertising: short, non-intrusive adverts shown at the result screen after each level, with a clear option to remove them permanently through the subscription. The three streams and their position in the model are shown in Figure 2.

6. Target Market and Demographics

Three distinct groups of players were identified through discussion and through the group's own knowledge of the Nepali media and gaming landscape.

The first and largest group is Nepali young adults between eighteen and twenty-eight who are active on social media, follow political news, and play casual browser or mobile games. This group is the core audience. They are familiar with the real public figures that the game's characters satirise, which makes the game immediately legible to them, and they are the group most likely to share the shareable exposé card that the game generates on level completion. Their share of the expected player base is estimated at fifty-three percent.

The second group is the Nepali diaspora, primarily in the United Kingdom, Australia, the United States, and the Gulf states. This group tends to follow Nepali politics with particular intensity because distance creates a kind of political nostalgia, and games that engage with home-country identity tend to travel well within diaspora communities once the first members of a community start sharing them. Their spending capacity is significantly higher than the domestic audience, and it is expected that the subscription and purchase conversion rate within this group will be two to three times the domestic rate. Their share of the player base is estimated at twenty-eight percent.

The third group is educators and civil society workers, including journalism students, NGO researchers, and civic education facilitators who may adopt the game as a classroom or workshop tool. This group is small in number but high in value, since an institutional adoption can bring fifty to two hundred players at once through a single partnership. Their share is estimated at nineteen percent of the base but a disproportionately larger share of total revenue. The breakdown is shown in Figure 1.

7. Marketing Strategy

The marketing plan was built on the principle that the game's subject matter does a significant share of the marketing work for free, provided it is placed in front of the right people at the right moment. Political satire and civic commentary spread on social media when they feel relevant and timely. The plan is structured around that observation rather than around a large advertising budget.

7.1 Content seeding

Short video clips of the game's most striking moments — the bribe rejection animation, the newspaper cinematic, the corruption network diagram — were planned for YouTube Shorts, TikTok, and Instagram Reels. These clips require no cost beyond the time to record and edit them. The shareable exposé card that the game generates automatically at the end of each successful level is designed to function as organic social content, since a player who posts their card is effectively creating a personalised advertisement for the game.

7.2 Creator partnerships

Nepali political commentary creators on YouTube were identified as the highest value channel. The game is a natural fit for a creator who already has an audience interested in Nepali governance and satire. Small partnerships, in which the game is shown during a video in exchange for a free supporter subscription rather than a cash fee, were planned as the primary outreach mechanism for the first two months. This keeps cost low while reaching an audience that is already primed by the creator's own framing.

7.3 Civil society and education channel

Direct outreach to journalism programmes at Tribhuvan University and at Softwarica College, to organisations such as Freedom Forum Nepal, and to civic education NGOs was planned as a parallel channel. An institutional access tier — offering ten player accounts for a flat monthly fee — would be introduced at month three to convert the interest from this channel into recurring income.

7.4 Paid acquisition

A modest paid acquisition budget was reserved for the third month of the launch plan, after retention data from the soft launch had been gathered and the cost-per-retained-player had been measured. Spending money to acquire players before that number is known would be a poor use of the budget. The goal of the paid spend is to lift the player base above the threshold at which organic growth begins to sustain itself, not to substitute for it.

9. Revenue Projection and Break-even

A revenue projection was built on a small set of stated assumptions. It was assumed that the soft launch produces an initial active base of 800 players in month one, growing to 3,500 by month six through the marketing push and organic sharing. A subscription conversion rate of 3.5 percent was assumed across the base, with an average monthly subscription revenue of NPR 299 per subscriber. An additional in-game purchase revenue of NPR 45 per active player per month was estimated based on comparable casual game benchmarks at low price points. Advertising revenue was assumed at NPR 12 per active player per month for the share of players not covered by subscriptions. The operating cost after launch was estimated at NPR 7,000 per month, covering hosting, backend, and a minimal maintenance allocation.

10. Risks and Ethics

10.1 Commercial risks

The most significant commercial risk is that retention falls below the assumed rate, which would slow the rise of the revenue line and push break-even beyond month six or beyond the projection entirely. This risk is mitigated by keeping the launch cost low and by using the soft launch period to measure actual retention before the main marketing spend is committed. A second risk is that the marketing channel — specifically the creator partnership approach — does not reach enough of the target audience to seed organic growth. This risk is mitigated by diversifying across the three channels described in Section 7. A third risk is the political sensitivity of the subject matter: a game that satirises specific public figures by name carries some possibility of legal challenge or of political pressure on distribution platforms. This risk was discussed with a legal adviser during planning, and the advice received was that satirical content about public figures acting in their public capacity is protected in the relevant jurisdictions, provided the character is clearly framed as satire rather than as factual accusation.

10.2 Ethical risks

The ethical complications of this business model are not trivial and must be stated plainly. The source shield, the deadline extension, and the evidence vault all earn money from the urgency that the game's own mechanics create. A player who buys a deadline extension is spending money in response to the time pressure that the game deliberately applied to them. This is structurally the same as the streak shield in any other freemium game, and the criticism that applies there applies here. The position taken by the group is that several constraints make the model acceptable. All purchases are optional, all content is reachable without spending, spending is capped within a session, and no purchase is shown during a moment of peak emotional pressure such as the loss screen. These constraints reduce revenue, and that reduction was accepted because the alternative — a design that maximises spending by maximising distress — is not consistent with the civic purpose of the product. The civic dimension of the game adds a further responsibility: a product that teaches young people about corruption should not itself use manipulative commercial practices. That standard was kept in view throughout the design of the monetisation layer.

12. Project Plan

The post-launch plan was structured across a six-month period beginning from the point of public launch. A soft launch in a limited community — specifically the Softwarica College student network and one or two creator audiences — was placed first, so that real retention and conversion data could be gathered before any significant marketing spend was made. The institutional outreach and the main marketing push were placed in months two and three, once the product had been tested in a live environment. The subscription tier would be introduced at month three, after enough players had experienced the game to understand what the subscription offers them. A formal break-even review was placed at month six. The plan is shown in Figure 5.

13. Conclusion

A business case for Parda Faas was set out that treats the daily engagement produced by the game's gamification layer as a commercial asset. A freemium model was selected, and three revenue streams were designed to sit within the game's civic investigative vocabulary rather than beside it as generic transactions. The target market was broken into three groups — domestic Nepali youth, the Nepali diaspora, and civil society educators — and a lean marketing plan was built around the content and channels most likely to reach each group at low initial cost. A launch cost breakdown in Nepali Rupees was prepared, with marketing as the largest item. A cautious revenue projection places break-even at approximately month six, with the projection depending on stated assumptions that would be checked against real data from a soft launch before the main spend is committed.

The ethical complications of the model were addressed directly. The conclusion drawn by the group is that a product with civic purpose can earn money without undermining that purpose, provided that the monetisation design applies the same discipline to commercial choices that the game itself applies to its subject matter. The test is simple: if the game teaches players to resist manipulation for public gain, it cannot itself manipulate players for private gain. That test was kept in view throughout, and the places where the group believes the model passes it — and the places where it requires ongoing vigilance — were stated honestly in Section 10.
